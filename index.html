Section 1Intro to Law & Policy

Sources of Law

  • There are 2 main sources of law: Statutory Law (legislation) and Common Law (case law)
  • Legal action can be criminal, civil, or disciplinary action
Civil Action

Plaintiff vs Defendant

Criminal Action

Prosecutor vs Accused

Australia's Constitutional Framework

  • Australia is the only liberal democracy not to have a bill of rights; instead, the Commonwealth of Australia Constitution Act lays powers from separation of government
  • Australians' rights come from international treaties and case law
  • A bill of rights is a list of fundamental rights and freedoms that a government promises to protect for its people
  • Local councils are not named in the Constitution but are delegated by states, whereas state/territory and federal parliaments are outlined in constitutional powers
  • Most laws are made by states
  • Queensland only has 1 lower house
  • A bill goes through 3 readings before being blessed with royal assent by the Governor-General and becoming an act of parliament

Health Policy & Resources

  • Health policy is largely instituted by governments
  • Some hospital policies will be in contradiction of the law, but you always need to abide by the law
  • Courts make decisions on the basis that healthcare resources are infinite (not realistic)
  • Rationing occurs at high levels (PBS) or at lower levels (admission of patients to certain beds) in healthcare to distribute limited resources

The Doctrine of Precedent

Doctrine of Precedent

Courts are bound to follow the decisions of courts higher up in the same hierarchy.

Regulatory Bodies

Key Bodies

The Medical Board of Australia and Australian Health Practitioner Regulation Agency (AHPRA) and the Queensland Office of the Health Ombudsman can investigate complaints about unsatisfactory professional practice and misconduct.

Section 2Human Rights Law

International Treaties & Conventions

  • Human rights are codified in international agreements or treaties between governments called conventions or covenants
  • By ratifying a treaty, a country voluntarily accepts legal obligations under international law
  • If a country does not follow a treaty, very little can be done as a consequence
  • The obligations under any international treaty are not binding within Australia unless and until they are incorporated into Australian domestic laws

Australia's Rights Framework

  • There is nothing in the Australian Constitution that guarantees the protection of human rights broadly
  • Queensland adopted the Human Rights Act 2019
Queensland Human Rights Act 2019 — Key Provisions
  • Everyone has the right to access health services without discrimination
  • A person must not be refused emergency medical treatment that is immediately necessary to save the person's life

Section 3Professionalism & Ethics

Professionalism in Medicine

Professionalism

The set of intrinsic values (respect, care, responsibility, accountability, self-awareness, integrity and clinical competence), expressed as extrinsic behaviours that justify trust between patients and doctors, and between the public and the medical profession.

  • The profession (medicine) sets its own standards and expectations
  • MBA's Code of Conduct: Good Medical Practice Guide

Ethics in Medicine

  • Ethics is a generic term for various ways of understanding and examining moral life

Virtue Ethics

Virtue Ethics

Moral action means acting in accordance with virtuous traits (intrinsic, established traits). E.g., an honest person tells the truth because to lie would be dishonest, and being an honest person and telling the truth is what they do.

Limitations of Virtue Ethics
  • How do you define a virtuous person?
  • No one is ALL virtuous
  • If the action is not easy, is that person not virtuous?
  • Cannot be applied consistently

Deontology

Deontology

Morality of an act is based on whether the act itself is right or wrong. An act is intrinsically, inherently right if it is good without qualification.

Limitations of Deontology
  • Consequences of an act cannot be used to decide if the actor had a good will
  • Individualistic
  • Where do those with limited capacity stand?
  • Does not consider the context or the consequences because of the focus on rational thought
  • Experiential understanding is not required

Utilitarianism

Utilitarianism

The morality of an act is based on whether the act produces the greatest good — a consequentialist or teleological theory. Conduct is judged in accordance with the consequences. Everyone's happiness is equal and of equal worth.

Limitations of Utilitarianism
  • Disregard for individual rights
  • Decisions made for the greatest good cannot create that same good for all — could disadvantage minorities
  • What about unintended consequences?

Bioethical Principles

The Four Principles

Autonomy · Beneficence · Non-maleficence · Justice — may conflict with each other and will often need to be balanced

Types of Justice

  • Distributive justice — allocation of health resources
  • Rights-based justice — patient rights
  • Legal justice — be accountable for acts or failures to act in accordance with morally just laws
Limitations of Bioethical Principles
  • Assumes the doctor is the person making decisions
  • Principal confliction can occur with no clear way to differentiate
AMA Code of Ethics

The AMA code of ethics is essential for setting and maintaining expected behavioural standards.

Section 4Adult Capacity & Consent

Informed Consent

Informed Consent

A person's voluntary decision about medical care that is made with knowledge and understanding of the benefits and risks involved.

  • Good medical practice involves providing information in a way patients can understand, obtaining informed consent, ensuring patients are informed about fees/charges, and advising patients about additional investigative costs
  • Consent is required to provide treatment for all adults and children — reflects the view that every person's body is inviolate and protects the autonomy and self-determination of the individual
  • Consent may be written, verbal, or implied but must be documented

Parts of Valid Consent

1
Decision-Making Capacity

The patient must have decision-making capacity

2
Freely & Voluntarily Given

Even firm advice is not undue influence

3
Sufficient Specificity

Consent must relate to the treatment — nature AND purpose in broad terms

Decision-Making Capacity

  • Adults 18+ have a presumption of capacity that can be rebutted
By Legislation

Capacity to make a decision means the person is capable of: understanding the nature and effect of the decision; freely and voluntarily making the decision; and communicating the decision in some way.

By Common Law

Capacity is the ability to understand, retain, believe, weigh, and communicate.

  • All patients must be informed about the nature and purpose of the treatment, the broad requirements of the treatment, and also the specific risks, benefits, alternatives, costs etc. to obtain valid and informed consent
  • Adult patients have the right to refuse life-sustaining medical treatment
  • Pregnant women have the right to refuse treatment and can refuse medical intervention that could save the foetus' life and/or their own
  • The mental health act cannot be deployed to achieve the detention of an individual against their will merely because their thinking process is unusual, even apparently bizarre and irrational, and contrary to the views of the overwhelming majority of the community at large
  • If a patient informs the doctor they want a form of treatment and the doctor concludes this is not clinically indicated, the doctor is not required to provide it — although they should offer to arrange a second opinion
  • A patient may have capacity for treatment A but not B

Advance Health Directive (AHD)

  • Mechanism for adults at a time when they have capacity to plan for and leave directions for their future care and treatment, should decision-making capacity be lost
  • Can leave wishes, specific directions, and/or appoint an attorney
  • AHD must be signed by the adult, eligible witness, and a doctor who attests that they have discussed the AHD and that the adult had capacity at the time of signing
  • Operates only when adult loses capacity
  • An adult with decision-making capacity can revoke an AHD or make a new one
  • A health provider is not affected by an adult's AHD to the extent the health provider does not know the adult has an AHD
AHD May Be Overridden If...

A health professional has reasonable grounds to believe the direction in an AHD is:

  • Uncertain
  • Inconsistent with good medical practice
  • Medical treatments have advanced to the point where the terms of the direction are inappropriate

QCAT

  • Queensland Civil and Administrative Tribunal — can appoint guardians to make health decisions for adults lacking capacity

Enduring Power of Attorney

  • The adult can appoint multiple enduring powers of attorney to make decisions in relation to financial and personal matters (including healthcare)
  • Multiple attorneys are joint attorneys unless otherwise stated — there is an obligation to regularly consult and they must exercise the power unanimously, unless the enduring document provides otherwise
  • If there is a disagreement, the public guardian may exercise power for the matter

Statutory Health Attorney

Order of priority:

  1. Close/continuing spouse
  2. Person >18 years who has care of the adult and is not a paid carer
  3. Person >18 years who is a close friend or relative and not a paid carer
  4. The Public Guardian

Information for Substitute Decision-Makers

Substitute decision-makers require information on:

  • The nature of the adult's condition
  • The particular form of healthcare
  • The reasons why
  • The alternative forms of healthcare available
  • The general nature and effect of each form of healthcare
  • The significant risks associated with each form of healthcare

Principles for Decision-Makers

General Principles
  • Presumption of capacity
  • Respect for a person's human rights
  • Empowering the adult to exercise their rights and freedoms
  • Maintaining the adult's existing supportive relationships
  • Respect for privacy
  • Maximising the adult's participation in decision-making
  • Making decisions in a way that safeguards the adult's best interests
Healthcare Principles
  • Non-discrimination
  • Considering the person's inherent dignity, worth and autonomy
  • If attorneys make a decision that is contrary to the principles, the public guardian steps in

Treatment Without Consent

  • If no consent is provided, treatment is unlawful unless it is urgent health care or minor/uncontroversial health care
  • Some health cares cannot be consented to by the patient or their representative and require approval from a court, including special health care and ECT
Healthcare Without Consent — Permitted When:
  • The provider reasonably believes that the adult has impaired capacity, AND
  • The healthcare should be carried out urgently to meet imminent risk to the adult's life or to prevent significant pain or distress, AND
  • It is not reasonably practicable to get consent from a substitute decision-maker
Healthcare CANNOT Be Carried Out Without Consent If:
  • Doctor knows the adult objects to it in an AHD
  • Provider knows the adult objects — unless the adult has minimal or no understanding of the healthcare
  • The provider must fully document in the patient's records the various things enabling the healthcare

Good Samaritan

Good Samaritan Legislation

Helping at the scene — you are not liable, providing they act in good faith, without gross negligence, and without fee or reward.

Decisions Reserved for the Adult / Court Only

Cannot Be Made by Substitute Decision-Makers

Some decisions can only be made by the adult themselves under AHD or by tribunal or supreme court:

  • Tissue donation
  • Sterilisation
  • Termination of pregnancy
  • Medical research participation
Benevolent Paternalism

The notion of doing it for the patient's own good.

Section 5Child Capacity

Presumptions & Parental Consent

  • Children are presumed NOT to have capacity to consent to healthcare
  • There is an expectation that parents will make informed decisions in their child's best interests
  • Only one parent is required to consent to routine medical treatments unless:
    • There is a court order requiring joint decision-making for major long-term issues, OR
    • The treatment is a special medical procedure which requires court or tribunal approval
  • If parents cannot agree, application to the Family Court happens — the court decides based on the best interests of the child
  • Parents do not have capacity to consent for procedures proposed for the primary purpose of sterilisation (not cancer or something else)

Special Medical Procedures

Special Medical Procedure — Defined By:
  • Invasive, irreversible nature
  • Significant risk of making the wrong decision
  • Consequences of a wrong decision would be particularly grave
  • Conflicting interests of the child, parents, carers, or other family

Other examples: organ donation, ethnically controversial issues (intersex child).

Court Override of Parental Refusal

  • If parents refuse to consent to treatment that is believed to be in the child's best interests, the courts can be asked to make a decision and can overrule the parents' refusal
  • No criminal liability is incurred by a practitioner administering a blood transfusion even where a parent has refused to consent, so long as:
    • A blood transfusion is necessary to preserve the life of the child, AND
    • A second medical practitioner examines the child and agrees

Gillick Competence

Gillick Competence

If a child has sufficient understanding and intelligence to understand fully what is proposed, they are Gillick competent and can provide consent to treatment (assessed on a case-by-case basis).

  • If a parent of a child is under 18 years of age themselves, the parent must have capacity to make decisions for the child — deemed Gillick competence
  • If the parent lacks capacity because they are not Gillick competent, a guardian may need to be appointed

What to Look for in Gillick Assessment

  • Do they understand the nature of the procedure?
  • Can they accurately explain it back?
  • Do they have insight into why the treatment is in their best interests?
  • Do they understand risks and benefits?
  • Have they considered alternatives?
  • Their ability to consider all aspects of care
  • Do they demonstrate maturity and autonomy in other aspects of life?
  • If in doubt — not competent
Even If Not Gillick Competent

The child's dignity and right to information should be respected.

Critical Rule

A Gillick competent child cannot refuse treatment that is otherwise in their best interests, including life-saving treatment. Courts can overrule a child's refusal and have a responsibility to protect children from harm until they reach adulthood.

Section 6Confidentiality & Privacy

Patient Rights & The Duty of Confidentiality

  • Patients have a right to expect confidential information is held by doctors and staff unless release is permitted by law
  • Patient has a right to know what information is held about them, their right to access their medical records, and their right to have control over its use and disclosure, with limited exceptions

Why Confidentiality Matters

  • Encourages patients to be frank and discuss sensitive matters freely
  • Protects a patient's autonomy
  • No risk of discrimination
  • Elimination of trust
Common Law Definition of Confidential Information

Information disclosed on the basis that it was to remain confidential. It is acquired by a person, in their capacity as the designated person, from which a person who is receiving or has received a public health service could be identified.

Cardinal Rule

ANYTHING THAT IS EXCHANGED BETWEEN YOU AND THE PATIENT IS CONFIDENTIAL.

Lawful Disclosures

It is lawful to disclose confidential information:

  • For care or treatment of a patient (treating team or colleague overtaking care)
  • At the consent of the patient
  • When there is risk to the public
  • When another law requires disclosure (child abuse, notifiable disease, legal proceedings, etc.)

Disclosure to Persons with Sufficient Interest

  • You are allowed to disclose to a person with sufficient interest — close relative, close friend, adult who is providing home care, or a GP who has had responsibility for care and treatment
  • BUT NOT when you are expressly told not to share that information
  • This obligation persists after a patient dies

Disclosure in the Public Interest

Requirements for Public Interest Disclosure
  • The relevant chief executive must authorise in writing
  • Only so far as necessary to prevent imminent serious harm
  • Disclosed to an appropriate responsible authority
  • Only in the most compelling circumstances

Privacy Law

  • Both Australian Privacy Principles (APPs) and Queensland Privacy Principles (QPPs) exist
  • APPs are for private practises
  • QPPs are for public practises
  • Oversight: Office of the Information Commissioner Queensland & Office of the Australian Information Privacy Commissioner

Queensland Privacy Principles (QPPs)

  • Manage personal information in an open and transparent way
  • Anonymity
  • Can only collect personal information that is reasonably necessary
  • Assess unsolicited personal information
  • Notify individuals about the collection of personal information
  • Can only use or disclose information for the purpose it was collected
  • Ensure information is up to date and accurate
  • Reasonable steps to ensure the protection of personal information
  • Give access to and correct personal information held

Personal vs Sensitive Information

Personal Information

An individual who is reasonably identifiable from the information or opinion.

Sensitive Information (additional protections)

Racial origin, political opinions, sexual orientation, criminal record, biometric information.

Privacy Tort (Cause of Action)

Cause of Action

An individual has a cause of action against another if the other person invaded the individual's privacy by intruding upon their seclusion or misusing information relating to them.

It is a defence if the other person acted with lawful authority or in certain circumstances involving consent, necessity, or the defence of persons or property.

Genetic Information

Permitted Health Situation — Genetic Information Disclosure

A permitted health situation for disclosure of genetic information exists if:

  • The organisation obtained the information in the course of providing a health service
  • The organisation reasonably believes that use or disclosure is necessary to lessen or prevent a serious threat to the life or health of a genetic relative
  • The disclosure is lawful
  • The relative is no further removed than 3rd degree
  • If disclosure is permissible but the health practitioner is unwilling to disclose due to conscience or extenuating circumstances within the family, they should consult another suitably qualified health practitioner and consider if it would be more appropriate coming from them

Section 7Negligence

Negligence

Causing damage to another through a failure to take reasonable care — can be an act or omission. Civil action: the plaintiff needs to prove these elements on the balance of probabilities.

The 4 Elements of a Negligence Action

1
Duty of Care Owed

A legal duty exists between parties

2
Standard & Breach of Duty

Failure to meet the expected standard

3
Harm & Causation

Recognised harm causally connected to the breach

4
Damages

Quantifiable and foreseeable

Critical Rule

All 4 elements are needed for a successful negligence case, commenced within 3 years of the damages occurring.

  • Doctor's duty of care: a single comprehensive duty covering all the ways in which a doctor is called upon to exercise skill and judgement
  • Vicarious liability: employer is vicariously liable for the negligence of employees for negligent acts and omissions arising in the course of employment
  • A hospital owes its patients a non-delegable duty of care — cannot pass the blame

Element 2: Standard & Breach of Duty

  • The standard of care is that expected of a reasonable and competent doctor in the same set of circumstances
  • Factors considered:
    • The risk must have been foreseeable
    • Not insignificant, serious harm
    • Burden of taking precautions
    • Whether a reasonable person would have taken the precautions
  • A doctor does not breach their duty if they acted in a way that was widely accepted at the time by peer professional opinion
  • However, if their actions contradict the written law or are labelled irrational, the peer professional opinion defence cannot be used

Duty to Inform of Material Risks

Material Risk

A risk is material if a reasonable person in the patient's position or the doctor's position would be likely to attach significance to it. The patient would also need to establish causation.

  • A healthcare professional has a duty to inform the patient of all material risks inherent in the treatment/intervention
  • A person is not liable for negligence for harm suffered as a result of the materialisation of an inherent risk — one that cannot be avoided by the exercise of reasonable care and skill

Element 3: Harm & Causation

  • A recognised form of harm has resulted and there has been some damage
  • The damage was causally connected to the breach (factual causation)
  • Consider if the scope of liability can extend to the person in breach
  • Despite the duty to warn of all material risks, a doctor will only be liable if the failure to warn was the cause of the harm (factual causation)
  • The scope of liability is about ensuring the patient is protected from the occurrence of a physical injury which is unacceptable to them
  • The patient will not be compensated for the occurrence of a physical injury were they prepared to take the risk

Element 4: Damages

  • Related to the degree of harm — NOT the degree of negligence
  • Types: general damages, specific damages, future losses and expenses
  • Compensation is designed to restore the plaintiff to the position they would have been in if the negligence had not occurred

Defences to Negligence

  • Contributory negligence — plaintiff contributed to the harm (e.g., failing to attend follow-up appointments, failure to properly describe symptoms)
  • Voluntary assumption of risk — patient voluntarily assumed a risk; no liability for materialisation of an inherent risk
  • Good Samaritan legislation — for those not at work in emergency situations
  • Employer will generally indemnify you against any alleged negligence

Special Rules

Failed Sterilisation / Contraceptive Cases

A court cannot award damages for economic loss arising out of the costs ordinarily associated with maintaining a child — failed sterilisation, failed contraceptive procedure/advice.

Criminal Negligence

Criminal negligence is rare and may occur where a person caused a death. Prosecution may be commenced for manslaughter and/or failure to provide the necessities of life.

Section 8Patient Safety & Open Disclosure

Adverse Events

Adverse Event

An unexpected and undesired incident directly associated with the care or services provided to the patient, caused by medical management. Includes the actions or omissions of individuals and can include systemic problems within the healthcare system. Sentinel = preventable.

Approaches to Learning from Adverse Events

Person Approach

Focuses on the negligent individual

Systems Approach

Focuses on upstream factors within the healthcare system

  • NSQHS (National Safety and Quality Health Standards) put in place to prevent systemic failures within the healthcare system causing harm to individuals

Good Medical Practice — Code of Conduct Principles

  • Care of patient is primary concern
  • Maintaining a high level of medical competence and professional conduct is essential
  • Working within scope of practice
  • Adequate knowledge and skills
  • Maintaining adequate records
  • Balancing benefit and harm
  • Providing treatment options
  • Alleviate patient symptoms and distress where a cure is possible or not possible
  • Supporting a patient's right to seek a second opinion
  • Effective use of resources

Clinical Incident Management Policy — Aims

  • Take proactive approaches to identify and treat hazards before patients are harmed
  • When patients are harmed, promptly intervene to minimise harm
  • Disclose the incident that resulted in harm
  • Ensure lessons are learned and communicated

Safety Culture Values

Openness Honesty Fairness Accountability Patient-centred care Reporting Training & Improvement

Open Disclosure

Open Disclosure — What It Involves

A core professional requirement and institutional obligation that includes:

  • An apology
  • A factual explanation
  • Opportunity for the patient/family to relay their experience
  • Discussion of the potential consequences
  • Explanation of the steps being taken to manage the adverse event and prevent recurrence
  • Litigation may be reduced if patients appreciate the fallibility and honesty of the clinician
Apology Does Not Equal Admission of Fault

An apology made by or on behalf of a person in relation to any matter alleged to have been caused by the person does not constitute an express or implied admission of fault or liability by the person in relation to the matter, and is not relevant to the determination of fault or liability in relation to the matter. Evidence of an apology is not admissible in any civil proceeding as evidence of fault or liability.

Section 9Termination of Pregnancy

Context

Despite decriminalisation around Australia, barriers to access remain a reality for many women.

Who May Perform / Consent to a Termination

  • A person who consents to, assists in, or performs a termination on themselves does not commit an offence
  • A medical practitioner may perform a termination on a person who is NOT MORE THAN 22 WEEKS pregnant

Termination After 22 Weeks

After 22 Weeks — Requirements

A medical practitioner may perform a termination if:

  • In all the circumstances, the practitioner believes that the termination should be performed, AND
  • The practitioner has consulted with another medical practitioner who also considers that, in all the circumstances, the termination should be performed

Relevant considerations include: all relevant medical conditions, future physical, psychological and social circumstances.

Emergency After 22 Weeks

Emergency Exception

In an emergency, a medical practitioner may perform a termination of pregnancy on someone who is more than 22 weeks pregnant if they consider it necessary to save the person's life or the life of another unborn child.

Conscientious Objection

  • A registered health practitioner may have a conscientious objection if asked to perform a termination of pregnancy
  • The practitioner must disclose their conscientious objection but is not required to explain why
  • The practitioner must refer the person or transfer their care to another registered medical practitioner who they believe can provide the requested service and who does not have a conscientious objection
  • This does not limit any duty owed in an emergency, regardless of conscientious objection
  • Prescribed students must disclose their conscientious objection to the relevant medical person

Consent & Legal Rights

Biological Father — No Legal Standing

Consent is not needed from the biological father, and he has no legal right to prevent or force a termination of pregnancy.

Adults with Impaired Capacity

QCAT or the Supreme Court must give consent for adults with impaired capacity to have an abortion — it is a special medical procedure.

Safe Access Zones

Safe Access Zones
  • Safe access zones around premises are 150 metres
  • Conduct is an offence if it:
    • Relates to terminations or could be perceived as relating to terminations
    • Is visible or audible to people entering or leaving
    • Would be reasonably likely to deter a person from entering or leaving, from requesting or undergoing a termination, or from performing or assisting with a termination
